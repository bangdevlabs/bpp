# Trademarks

**B++**, **Bang 9**, and **BangDev** are trademarks of Daniel Obino / BangDev.
The `./bpp` compiler logo (when published) is also a trademark.

The names follow a deliberate historical lineage: **B++** is a homage
to **B** (Ken Thompson, Bell Labs 1969, the direct ancestor of C);
**Bang 9** is a homage to **Plan 9 from Bell Labs** (Rob Pike et al,
1992, the minimalist Unix successor). Both are acknowledgments of
intellectual debt, not claims on the historical names — "B", "Plan 9",
and "Bell Labs" remain what they are.

## Permitted uses (fair use)

You may freely:

- **Refer to "B++"** when writing tutorials, blog posts, documentation,
  or any content describing the language or this project.
- **State that a program was written in B++** in your project's README
  or marketing materials (e.g. "Built with B++").
- **Quote code, documentation, and examples** from this repository
  under the terms of the Apache 2.0 license.
- **Run comparisons** between B++ and other tools, compilers, or
  languages for educational or review purposes.
- **Create "B++ compatible" content** — sprite packs, tutorials,
  educational videos — without seeking permission.

## Uses that require explicit permission

You may **not**, without prior written authorization from the
trademark holder:

- **Name a fork, derivative, or re-implementation** using "B++",
  "Bang 9", or any confusingly similar variant (e.g. "B+++", "BPlus",
  "Bang10", "BangNine", "bangfire"). Forks are welcome — just pick
  a distinct name.
- **Ship a compiler binary** named `bpp` or derivatives, claiming
  or implying that it is the canonical B++ compiler, when it is not.
- **Use the project's logo, icon, or visual branding** in your own
  product, company, or commercial material.
- **Register domain names, social media handles, or trademarks**
  that incorporate "B++", "Bang 9", or "BangDev".
- **Offer commercial products or services** named with the
  trademarks in a way that implies official endorsement (e.g.
  "B++ Professional Support", ""Bang 9 Enterprise"").

If in doubt, the safe path is: **use a different name for your
project**, then describe what it does (e.g. "a fork of B++",
"a B++-compatible compiler") without taking the name itself.

## Why this matters

B++ is distributed freely and openly. The value of the trademarks
is to keep **one canonical meaning** for the word "B++" — so that
a user downloading "B++" knows what they are getting, so that
tutorials and documentation all refer to the same language, and
so that the direction of the language is not diluted by
incompatible forks adopting the same name.

This is the same approach used by Rust, Go, Python, Swift, and
most major open-source language projects: **code is open, the
name is protected**.
